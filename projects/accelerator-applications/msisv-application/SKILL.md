## What MSISV Is and What They Care About (WIFM)

MSISV - Morgan Stanley Inclusive & Sustainable Ventures - is Morgan Stanley's flagship impact accelerator. It is not a typical startup program. It is Morgan Stanley's proof point that capital reaching underrepresented founders and mission-driven companies can generate both financial returns AND measurable social impact.

**What Morgan Stanley gets out of this (their WIFM):**

1. **ESG and regulatory credibility.** Morgan Stanley manages trillions in assets. Institutional investors increasingly require ESG accountability. MSISV is part of their documented commitment to inclusive capital deployment. Every cohort company is a data point they report to regulators, shareholders, and clients.

2. **Brand and talent differentiation.** Morgan Stanley competes for the best talent globally. MSISV signals to recruits - especially younger, values-driven professionals - that MS is more than a bank. The program has been running 8+ years because it delivers on this.

3. **Early deal flow in underserved markets.** They see companies before other VCs do. Founders who have faced systemic barriers often don't have Sequoia warm intros. MSISV is their front door. The $250K investment is small by MS standards - the real value is proprietary access to founders building in markets others ignore.

4. **Portfolio companies that validate their thesis.** Of 100+ companies scaled through MSISV, many have gone on to raise significant follow-on funding. That track record justifies the program internally and externally.

5. **Impact metrics they can publish.** Students supported, jobs created, dollars redirected to underserved communities. MSISV reviewers need to be able to tell a concrete impact story about every company they accept.

**What this means for every answer Efrat writes:**

The reviewer is not just evaluating Boostability. They are evaluating whether Boostability makes MSISV look good. Every answer should make it easy for the reviewer to answer two questions:

- "Can I tell a compelling impact story about this company?"
- "Will this founder succeed and reflect well on us?"

Frame answers to serve both questions. Never lead with features. Always lead with who benefits and why it matters at scale.

## The Five Things MSISV Scores On

Use these as a lens on every answer. Weak coverage on any one is a red flag.

**1. Founder story and underrepresented background**
MSISV exists because <2% of VC goes to women and multicultural founders. Efrat's profile - solo woman founder, immigrant, ADHD and raising children with ADHD, building for the students most overlooked by every system - is exactly the story they want to fund. This should be present in any answer that touches on founder motivation, team, or fit.

**2. Social impact clarity**
Who is being helped, how specifically, and how does it scale? Vague impact ("students do better") will lose to specific impact ("students who were about to drop out now submit assignments on time and stay enrolled"). The more precise the beneficiary and the outcome, the stronger the answer.

**3. Traction as proof of mission**
MSISV does not expect unicorn numbers at this stage. But they want evidence that the mission is working, not just theorized. A signed paid institutional contract with a chain of 14,000 students is strong traction. Frame traction as proof of impact, not just proof of product-market fit.

**4. Scalability and market size**
They are investing $250K expecting a path to something meaningful. Every answer about the model, the revenue, or the roadmap should make it clear that what works for 100 students can work for 100,000 - and why Boostability is the one to get there.

**5. Business model sustainability**
Impact that requires constant subsidy is not investable. Answers about revenue, burn, and model should show that Boostability gets more profitable as it grows - not more dependent. The Year 2 renewal dynamic (92%+ margins, zero re-acquisition cost) is a powerful answer to this.
